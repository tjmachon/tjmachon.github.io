2.2 Example Lagrangian systems

Here is the basic recipe for doing Lagrangian mechanics.

1.
Write down appropriate generalised coordinates q = (q1,q2,…,qN) for the system.
2.
Write down the kinetic and potential energies, K and V in terms of q and q⋅, to give the Lagrangian L = K − V .
3.
Solve the Euler-Lagrange equations (or if L has a special symmetry, solve a simpler first order equation).

We will now give a number of example applications of this procedure.

Example. Two dimensional particle of mass m in a central potential V (r). We use polar coordinates (r,𝜃). Then the kinetic energy is

K = 1 2m(r⋅2 + r2𝜃⋅2)

so the Lagrangian is

L = K − V = 1 2m(r⋅2 + r2𝜃⋅2) − V (r)

Because we have two coordinates r and 𝜃, we get two Euler-Lagrange equations. The Lagrangian does not depend on 𝜃, so the 𝜃 equation gives

d dtmr2𝜃⋅ = 0.

You should recognise this as the conservation of angular momentum p𝜃 = mr2𝜃⋅ in a central potential. Now we can look at the radial coordinate. The Euler-Lagrange equation is

mr̈ = mr𝜃⋅2 − V ′

Since we know that p𝜃 = mr2𝜃⋅ is constant, we can eliminate the 𝜃⋅ term to find

mr̈ = 1 mr3p𝜃2 − V ′.

Given a particular V (r), this can then be solved (e.g. V (r) = kr2). Of interest is whether the system admits uniform circular motion – that is motion with constant angular velocity and fixed radius r0. Then r̈ = r⋅ = 0. For a given r with V ′ > 0 this can always be achieved by solving

r0 = p𝜃2∕3 m1∕3(V ′(r0))1∕3.

For an attractive potential like V (r) = kr2, V ′(r) is always positive, so we find orbits at any radius, determined by the angular momentum as r0 = p𝜃(km)−1∕4

Example. Simple pendulum. We have a pendulum of fixed length L, mass m as shown in Figure 13. The pendulum is completely described by the angle 𝜃. The kinetic energy is given by

K = 1 2mL2𝜃⋅2

and the potential energy is the height. Assuming the height is zero when the pendulum points vertically downwards we find

V = mgL(1 − cos𝜃)

The Euler-Lagrange equation is then

−mgLsin𝜃 − mL2𝜃̈ = 0

which is the pendulum equation. Solving this analytically is difficult, and we will not do it here. Using the small angle approximation reduces this to simple harmonic motion.

Example. The two-body problem. We have two particles of masses m1, m2 and positions r1 = (x1,y1,z1) and r2 = (x2,y2,z2), each exerting a force on the other. These are equal and opposite by Newton’s third law.

The force experienced by particle 1, F1 is a function of their mutual displacement, F1(r1 −r2) and the force experience by particle 2 is F2 = −F1.

We will assume the force is conservative, so there is a potential function V (r1 −r2) so that

F1 = −∂V ∂r1,F2 = −∂V ∂r2

Note that this automatically takes care of Newton’s third law using the chain rule. In terms of the positions r1 and r2 the Lagrangian is

L = 1 2 m1 r⋅1 2 + m 2 r⋅2 2 − V (r 1 −r2)

We simplify the system by introducing the displacement R = r1 −r2 as a new generalised coordinate, so that V = V (R). We then need a second generalised coordinate. One choice would be the sum, r1 + r2, but it is more convenient to use the centre of mass r̄ defined as

r̄ = m1r1 + m2r2 m1 + m2

We can then write r1 and r2 in terms of R and r̄ as

r1 = r̄ + m2 m1 + m2R,r2 = r̄ − m1 m1 + m2R

We can then write the Lagrangian in terms of these new coordinates as

L = 1 2 (m1 + m2)|r̄⋅|2 + μ|R⋅|2 − V (R)

where μ = (m1m2)∕(m1 + m2) is the reduced mass. We then get six Euler-Lagrange equations, one for each component of r̄ and R, but we can package them into two sets, for r̄ and R. The Euler-Lagrange equation for the centre of mass coordinate gives

0 = ∂L ∂r̄ − d dt ∂L ∂r̄⋅ = −d dt m1 + m2 2 r̄⋅

so that the centre of mass momentum p̄ = m1+m2 2 r̄⋅ is conserved. The equation for the displacement is more interesting, and we find

0 = ∂L ∂R − d dt ∂L ∂R⋅ = −∂V ∂R − μR̈

and we end up with the equation of motion

R̈ = −1 μ ∂V ∂R

note that

∂V ∂R = ∂V ∂R1, ∂V ∂R2, ∂V ∂R3.

We find the equation for the displacement is just a one-body problem with potential V and mass μ. If we can solve this one-body problem then we have solved the two-body problem. Commonly V depends only on the magnitude of the relative displacement, V = V (|r1 −r2|), as in gravitational interactions for example, in this case the model reduces to a central potential, which one can then split into angular and radial pieces (as we did in the two-dimensional case) and simplify the motion even further

Example. The double pendulum.

Consider the double pendulum in Figure 14

We can describe it with two coordinates, 𝜃1 and 𝜃2. We now need to write down the Lagrangian. The position of the mass m1 is given by

R1 = (ρ1 sin𝜃1,−ρ1 cos𝜃1),

so that the Lagrangian of mass 1 is given by

L1 = m1 2 ρ12(𝜃⋅ 1)2 + m 1gρ1 cos𝜃1.

The position of mass 2 is given by

R2 = R1 + (ρ2 sin𝜃2,−ρ2 cos𝜃2)

the kinetic energy of mass 2 is given by m2(R⋅2 ⋅R⋅2)∕2, and we get the Lagrangian for mass 2 as

L2 = m2 2 ρ12(𝜃⋅ 1)2 + 2ρ 1ρ2 cos(𝜃1 − 𝜃2)𝜃⋅1𝜃⋅2 + ρ22(𝜃⋅ 2)2 + m 2g(ρ1 cos𝜃1 + ρ2 cos𝜃2)

The total Lagrangian of this system is then

L = L1 + L2

The resulting Euler-Lagrange equations are very complex, and display chaotic behaviour, which we will talk about later in the course. Trying to write the equations of motion down without Lagrangian mechanics would have been nearly impossible.

Example. Relativistic point particle. Einstein showed us that Newton’s laws are not exact, and we can no longer use the kinetic and potential energies for our Lagrangian. Recall that the general form of the Euler-Lagrange equations, in terms of generalised forces and momenta is

F = d dtp

Now what is the relativistic form of the momentum? Einstein calculated that

p = mv⋅ 1 − |v |2 ∕c2

This can be written as

p = ∂ ∂v − mc21 − (v 2 ∕c2 )

hence we we find the Lagrangian

L = −mc21 − (v 2 ∕c2 ) − V

where we assume the potential V generates the force. We can recover the non-relativistic limit by expanding

−mc21 − (v 2 ∕c2 ) ≈−mc2 + 1 2mv2,

the − mc2 term is constant and so can be ignored. Hence we find

L = 1 2mv − V.

Now assume V = 0. We know from special relativity that the proper time of a particle τ is defined as

−c2τ2 = x2 − c2t2

and is Lorentz invariant – τ records the time measured on a clock carried by the particle. The infinitesimal version of this gives

−c2(dτ)2 = (dx)2 − c2(dt)2

so that dτ = 1 − (v 2 ∕c2 )dt, and we find that

ℒ = ∫ −mc2dτ.

One final note, normally in the calculus of variations we are allowed to choose any path for our particle. But in this case, if v is faster than the speed of light, the functional diverges. Hence we are restricted to paths only in the light-cone of our particle, Figure 15, the paths that respect causality.

Example. Classical charged particles. Consider a particle of charge e, mass m, moving under the Lorentz force with position r = (x,y,z) = (r1,r2,r3).

F = e(E + r⋅ ×B)

for electric field E and magnetic field B. The kinetic energy of the particle is just

K = m 2 r⋅2.

To find a Lagrangian description (with q = r) of this system we need to find a potential energy V (r,r⋅) which produces the Lorentz equations. E and B are related to the electromagnetic potentials A and ϕ by the relations

E = −∇ϕ −∂A ∂t ,B = ∇×A

or

Ei = −∂ϕ ∂ri −∂Ai ∂t ,Bi = 𝜖ijk∂Aj ∂rk

where we have used Einstein notation (index summation). Now consider the expression

V = eϕ − er⋅ ⋅A

putting it into the Euler-Lagrange equations we find

∂V ∂ri − d dt ∂V ∂r⋅j = e ∂ϕ ∂ri − er⋅j∂Aj ∂ri + e d dtAi

or

∂V ∂ri − d dt ∂V ∂r⋅j = e ∂ϕ ∂ri − er⋅j∂Aj ∂ri + e∂Ai ∂t + ∂2Ai ∂rj∂r⋅j

Now we have the vector calculus identity

(a ×∇×b)i = 𝜖ijkaj𝜖klm∂bm ∂rl = −aj ∂bi ∂rj + aj∂bj ∂ri

which tells us that

∂V ∂ri − d dt ∂V ∂r⋅j = −eE −r⋅ ×B = −F

adding in the kinetic energy we find the full Lagrangian

L = m 2 r⋅2 − eϕ + er⋅ ⋅A

Note that under a gauge transformation

A↦A + ∇χ,ϕ↦ϕ −χ⋅

the Lagrangian is not invariant, but the equations of motion are.
