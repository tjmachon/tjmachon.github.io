1.4 Forces, Lagrangians and the second law

How do we incorporate forces into our theory? Staying in one dimension for now, many forces can be written as the gradient of a potiential V , as

F = −∂V ∂x

The potential energy of the system is then equal to V . In this particular case we can easily incorporate the force into our framework.

Definition. For a non-relativistic classical system, the Lagrangian L is defined as

L = K − V

where K is the kinetic and V the potential energy.

Note that for some forces, like friction, which arise as effective descriptions of microscopic interactions, it is not possible to write down a potential energy, so there is no Lagrangian. We will discuss such cases later. Nevertheless, the fundamental forces of physics such as electromagnetism and gravity can be written this way.

Example. For a mass m attached to a harmonic spring with spring constant k the Lagrangian is

L = 1 2mx⋅2 −k 2x2

Definition. For a system with Lagrangian L, the action is defined as

S = ∫ Ldt

We have left the limits on this integral ambiguous, but we are always thinking of some specified time interval, with given boundary conditions.

Proposition. (Hamilton’s principle of stationary action). The motion of a classical particle with Lagrangian L extremises (is a stationary point of) the action S.

Here we will give the proof only for a one-dimensional system. We will introduce the full theory later. Using our ideas from the calculus of variations, we know that any motion extremising the action will satisfy the Euler-Lagrange equations

∂L ∂x − d dt ∂L ∂x⋅ = 0

The second term just gives mẍ = p⋅ (where p is the momentum), as before. But now the first term gives us forces. We can compute

∂L ∂x = −∂V ∂x = F

putting it together we get

F − d dtp = 0,orF = mẍ,

Newton’s second law.

Hamilton’s principle is equivalent to the statement that, of all the possible trajectories in configuration space a system may take between the fixed initial and final configurations, the one particular path consistent with the laws of mechanics is the one for which the functional derivative of the action vanishes. This is remarkable.

Let’s do a simple example.

Example. Simple harmonic motion. Consider a one-dimensional particle of mass m, attached to a wall by a spring of spring constant k. The kinetic energy K is equal to

K = 1 2mx⋅2

and the potential energy

V = 1 2kx2

so the Lagrangian is equal to

L = 1 2mx⋅2 −1 2kx2

and the Euler-Lagrange equations give simple harmonic motion

mẍ = −kx
