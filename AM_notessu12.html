2.3 Constraints in Lagrangian mechanics

The pendulum systems we considered above had an implicit constraint that the pendulum length was fixed. Sometimes, however it is not possible to incorporate the constraints in such a natural way.

Definition. A constraint on a mechanical system with generalised coordinates q is called holonomic if it can be written as

g(q,t) = 0

for some function g.

Note that holonomic constrains depend only on the generalised coordinates, not the velocities. There is a further distinction on holonomic constraints between scleronomic where g has no explicit time-dependence and rheonomic where it does.

Definition. A constraint on a mechanical system with generalised coordinates q is called non-holonomic if it can be written as

f(q,q⋅,t) = 0

for some function f with explicit dependence on q⋅.

We first consider holonomic constraints.

Example. Pendulum. Suppose we have a pendulum of length ρ, then the position of the mass is given by q = (x(t),y(t)). The constraint is

x(t)2 + y(t)2 − ρ2 = 0

so the constraint function is written as g(q,t) = g((x(t),y(t)),t) = x(t)2 + y(t)2 − ρ2 and has no explicit t dependence.

How do we incorporate constraints into our system. Consider a system with two generalised coordinates q = (q1,q2) and Lagrangian L. Then Hamilton’s variational principle states

∫ δL δq1δq1 + δL δq2δq2 dt = 0.

Previously we were allowed to choose the variations δq1(t) and δq2(t) independently, so we got two separate equations by setting each functional derivative to zero. Now suppose that there is a constraint g(q1,q2,t), the variations can no longer be chosen independently, they must satisfy

∂g ∂q1δq1 + ∂g ∂q2δq2 = 0

Now consider the time-dependent vectors

δq = δq1,δq2 ,δL = δL δq1, δL δq2 ,η = ∂g ∂q1, ∂g ∂q2

Our variational principle with constraints can now be stated as

δL ⋅ δq = 0,forallδqsuchthatη ⋅ δq = 0

This implies that

δL = λ(t)η

for some function λ(t). Hence we find the Euler-Lagrange equations for holonomic constraints with any number of generalised coordinates

∂L ∂qi − d dt ∂L ∂q⋅i = λ(t) ∂g ∂qi

with λ(t) chosen to enforce the constraint. Note the similarity to the constrained calculus of variations formulae.

Example. Ball rolling down a slope without slipping. Suppose a ball of radius r, mass m and moment of inertia I rolls down a slope of angle ϕ without slipping, as shown in Figure 16. We describe it with two coordinates s and 𝜃. Because the ball rolls without slipping we have the constraint

s − r𝜃 = 0

then the kinetic energy is

K = 1 2 ms⋅2 + I𝜃⋅2

and the potential energy is

V = −mgssinϕ

giving a Lagrangian

L = 1 2 ms⋅2 + I𝜃⋅2 + mgssinϕ

our constrained equations of motion are then

mgsinϕ − ms̈ = λ

I𝜃̈ = rλ

The constraint s − r𝜃 = 0 can be differentiated to give s̈ − r𝜃̈ = 0, using our equations of motion we find

gsinϕ − 1 mλ −r2 I λ = 0

which can be solved to give a constant value λ

λ = −gmsin𝜃 1 + mr2 I .

Now we consider general non-holonomic constraints. These are more difficult to deal with, and we will not study them in detail in this course.

Example. A unicycle. Consider a unicycle as shown in Figure 17, which rolls without slipping. It is described by the coordinates (x,y) of the contact point of the tyre with the ground, the angles 𝜃 and ϕ of the wheel. There are two constraints in this system. The change in 𝜃 is determined by the motion of the contact point, since the unicycle rolls without slipping, but the direction is determined by ϕ, so we find

x⋅ = ρ𝜃⋅cosϕ

y⋅ = ρ𝜃⋅sinϕ

which are non-holonomic constraints, since they depend on velocity.

There are other types of constraint which can be written as an inequality. For example, one might require z ≥ 0, i.e. the height of a particle is required to be greater than zero. Such constraints are also known as non-holonomic.
