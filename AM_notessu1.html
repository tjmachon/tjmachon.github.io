1.1 Newton’s first law revisited

We are all familiar with Newton’s first law. The translation from the Principia by Bernard Cohen1 reads:

Every body preserves its state of being at rest or of moving uniformly straight forward, insofar as it is compelled to change its state by forces impressed.

In other words, in the absence of a net force, particles move along straight lines at constant speed. But what is a straight line? I will give you two definitions; the first is local, the second is global.

1. A straight line segment is the trajectory traced out by a particle moving in a constant direction.
2. A straight line segment is the shortest path between two nearby points.

The technical term for “straight lines” in arbitrary spaces (e.g. the surface of a sphere), is geodesic – for two nearby points on a curved (or flat) space, the path of shortest distance between them is a geodesic. Geodesics occur in general relativity, so this is a good warmup. Note the “nearby” in the above definition.

When we think about the laws of physics, we normally think in a manner similar to definition 1 above: we have some initial condition (the position and velocity of a particle) and we evolve it according to a particular law of physics (here Newton’s laws). To be precise, let q(t) = (x(t),y(t),z(t)) be the position of a particle, and q⋅(t) = (x⋅(t),y⋅(t),z⋅(t)) the velocity. Then in a world without forces, Newton’s first law says (mathematically)

d dtq = q⋅(t), d dtq⋅(t) = 0.

We could instead use definition 2 for a straight line, and in this case Newton’s first law would tell us

In the absence of external forces, a particle moving between two nearby points A and B takes the path of shortest length, out of all possible paths, and does so at constant speed.

This is a fundamentally different way of thinking about physics. Rather than taking a local perspective, we have a global picture. We have a start point A and an end point B; and we say that the path taken by the particle is the one with the shortest distance out of all possible paths. How does it know? This perspective caused a great deal of philosophical consternation when first discovered. In this course we will learn how this perspective applies to all of classical mechanics (and beyond).
