2.7 Lagrangian mechanics and Feynman’s path integral

The analogue of Lagrangian mechanics in quantum systems is Feynman’s path integral. Suppose we have a classical particle located at q0(t0) and q1(t1) then we know that the path it takes is the path, out of all paths satisfying the boundary conditions, that minimises the action

S = ∫ t0t1 Ldt.

What can we say for a quantum particle. Suppose we have a quantum particle with classical Lagrangian L is located at q0(t0). What is the probability amplitude P that we find it at q1(t1) at time t1? We write this P in Dirac notation as

P = ⟨q0|Û(t0,t1)|q1⟩

where U(t0,t1) is the time evolution operator. Feynman, following Dirac, showed that this is given by

⟨q0|Û(t0,t1)|q1⟩ = ∫ allpaths exp i ℏS

where S is the classical action and ‘all paths’ refers to all possible paths between q0 and q1. Making such an integral rigorous is not easy, however we can proceed in ‘hand-waving’ fashion. Suppose ℏ is very small, then the integral oscillates wildly for all paths except near the path of least action, where the integral oscillates most slowly. Therefore, in the classical limit, ℏ → 0, all paths except the classical one (least action) have vanishingly small probability to occur. In other words, Lagrangian mechanics emerges as the classical limit of Feynman’s path integral.
