1.2 Global formulation of Newton’s first law

We argued that Newton’s first law can be phrased globally as:

In the absence of external forces, a particle moving between two nearby points A and B takes the path of shortest length, out of all possible paths, and does so at constant speed.

The goal of this section is to formulate this law in a precise mathematical form.

To do so we need to define the length of a particle’s trajectory. For now we will assume we are in two dimensions, so that the trajectory of the particle can be parameterised as

q(t) = (x(t),y(t)),

at each t, q(t) gives the x and y positions of the particle. For example, circular motion of radius R and angular frequency ω would be

q(t) = Rcosωt,Rsinωt.

We want to derive an expression for the distance travelled by a particle over a time T (if the trajectory is curved this is longer than the distance between the start and end points of the trajectory)2). We need only basic observations about the particle’s trajectory. The velocity of the particle is given by

q⋅(t) = (x⋅(t),y⋅(t)),

and the speed of the particle is given by

q⋅ = x⋅ 2 + y⋅ 2.

We know from mechanics the law

Distance = Speed ×Time,

this tells as that in a short time dt, the distance travelled, dl is equal to

dl = |q⋅|dt.

Then in a time T, the total distance travelled, ℒ must be the integral of dl, so we can write

ℒ = ∫ 0ℒdl = ∫ 0T q⋅dt = ∫ 0T dx dt 2 + dy dt2dt.

The middle expression above holds for any trajectory, in any number of dimensions.

Now suppose we have a particle travelling between A = (0,0) and B = (xT ,yT ) in time T. If we believe a straight line is the shortest path between A and B, then our global statement of Newton’s first law says that, in the absence of forces, the trajectory of a particle satisfying q(t) = (x(t),y(t)) with q(0) = A and q(T) = B will minimise the integral ℒ.

The above argument captures the “straight line” aspect. However, Newton’s law doesn’t only say that particles go in constant direction, but at constant speed. For example, suppose the motion is confined to the x axis. Then the two trajectories

q1(t) = xT t T,0,q2(t) = xT t2 T2,0

both give a straight line in the (x,y) plane, but the speed is different (this is like walking the same path but with a different speed). Only the first one is physical, how do we make this mathematical?

Recall the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality

∫ 0T f(t)dt2 ≤ T∫ 0T f(t)2dt

which is the integral version of (a + b)2 ≤ 2(a2 + b2). We can rewrite this as

a + b 2 2 ≤ a2 + b2 2

or ‘the square of the average is less than the average of the squares’. The inequality is an equality only if f(t)2 is constant. Applying this to our problem we find, for an arbitrary trajectory q(t):

ℒ2 ≤ T∫ 0T q⋅2dt.

Now suppose we try to find the trajectory q(t) from A to B that minimises the right hand side of the above inequality. We can always reduce the value of integral by making the speed is constant, but keeping the path the same, and if we do that it is equal to ℒ2∕T. We can then further reduce the value of the integral by choosing the path of least length. So, if we minimise the right-hand side we find the minimum length and constant speed – both parts of Newton’s first law. Now we can multiply the integral by a factor m∕2 without affecting anything and define the action.

Definition. In the absence of forces the action of a particle is the integral of its kinetic energy

S = ∫ 0T m 2 q⋅2dt.

and we find the following result.

Newton’s First Law (Global). In the absence of forces a particle traces out a trajectory q(t) between A and B that minimises the action (total kinetic energy).

We have found a direct link between Newton’s first law, kinetic energy, and geodesics. In other words, Newton’s first law states that in the absence of any force, particles go from A to B in such a way to minimise their total kinetic energy. Or in the words of Feynman:3

“As an example, say your job is to start from home and get to school in a given length of time with the car. You can do it several ways: You can accelerate like mad at the beginning and slow down with the brakes near the end, or you can go at a uniform speed, or you can go backwards for a while and then go forward, and so on. The thing is that the average speed has got to be, of course, the total distance that you have gone over the time. But if you do anything but go at a uniform speed, then sometimes you are going too fast and sometimes you are going too slow. Now the mean square of something that deviates around an average, as you know, is always greater than the square of the mean; so the kinetic energy integral would always be higher if you wobbled your velocity than if you went at a uniform velocity. So we see that the integral is a minimum if the velocity is a constant (when there are no forces).”
